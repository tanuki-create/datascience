Abstract
Contemporary
graph-based
retrieval-
augmented
generation
(RAG)
methods
typically begin by extracting entities from
user queries and then leverage pre-constructed
knowledge graphs to retrieve related relation-
ships and metadata. However, this pipeline’s
exclusive reliance on entity-level extraction
can lead to the misinterpretation or omission of
latent yet critical information and relations. As
a result, retrieved content may be irrelevant or
contradictory, and essential knowledge may be
excluded, exacerbating hallucination risks and
degrading the fidelity of generated responses.
To address these limitations, we introduce
PankRAG, a framework that combines a
globally aware, hierarchical query-resolution
strategy with a novel dependency-aware
reranking
mechanism.
PankRAG
first
constructs
a
multi-level
resolution
path
that captures both parallel and sequential
interdependencies within a query, guiding large
language models (LLMs) through structured
reasoning. It then applies its dependency-aware
reranker to exploit the dependency structure
among resolved sub-questions, enriching and
validating retrieval results for subsequent
sub-questions. Empirical evaluations demon-
strate that PankRAG consistently outperforms
state-of-the-art approaches across multiple
benchmarks, underscoring its robustness and
generalizability.

Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) exhibit exceptional
performance across diverse tasks by leveraging
pre-training on massive text corpora to generate
appropriate responses. While LLMs’ capabilities
can be further enhanced through fine-tuning on
downstream tasks, finite parameter capacity limits
their ability to store comprehensive and evolving
world knowledge. Retrieval-augmented generation
(RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020; Gao
et al., 2023; Ram et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024; Izac-
ard et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024)has emerged as the
predominant solution, owing to its straightforward
implementation. It enables static models to access
up-to-date external knowledge while preserving
interpretability and provenance tracking.
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) methods
dynamically integrate heterogeneous knowledge
bases (Sun et al., 2023; Edge et al., 2024; Guo et al.,
2024; Ma et al., 2024) to enhance contextual rea-
soning and factual grounding. Among these, knowl-
edge graphs (Chen et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2024)
serve as structured frameworks that encode infor-
mation through interconnected entities and relation-
ships, explicitly modeling semantic dependencies
to enable human-like understanding and multi-hop
logical inference. Recently, many approaches have
integrated RAG with knowledge graphs. For ex-
ample, GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) enhances
private-text question answering by precomputing
community summaries for clusters of related en-
tities, dynamically synthesizing these summaries
into contextually unified responses during query
processing. LightRAG (Guo et al., 2024) em-
ploys a graph-text hybrid architecture that embeds
structural relationships into document indexing and
retrieval. Its hierarchical retrieval system com-
bines graph-based relational reasoning with vec-
tor similarity matching, enabling efficient knowl-
edge extraction while preserving semantic fidelity
in multi-hop queries.
However, current graph-based RAG methods
exhibit critical limitations of inaccuracies in the
interpretation of critical information or relation-
ships when handling specific and abstract com-
plex queries.
Specific complex queries (SCQ)
demand entity-centric granularity, requiring pre-
cise retrieval of discrete entities and their rela-
tional contexts within structured knowledge sys-
tems, e.g., "In which direction would you travel to
reach Southampton from the city connected to it by

rail?". For specific queries, existing methods fail to
capture latent information embedded in multi-hop
relationships. For example, when GraphRAG’s
local search retrieves content for the prior spe-
cific query, it returns extensive information about
“Southampton,” most of which, beyond the city’s
geographic location and its rail-connected neigh-
bor, is irrelevant and may exacerbate LLM hal-
lucinations. Abstract complex queries(ACQ) em-
phasize conceptual synthesis beyond entity-level
precision, addressing domain-wide implications
or systemic interactions, e.g., “How can theoret-
ical principles from programming language the-
ory be made accessible to new developers?”,(A
detailed case study analyzing this example ACQ
is presented in subsection A.3). For ACQ, meth-
ods often overlook the implicit context underlying
conceptual abstractions. When LightRAG retrieves
content for the prior ACQ, it prioritizes formal
syntax definitions, while teaching tools, like step-
by-step tutorials, error-driven case studies, and di-
verse code examples—provides richer contextual
guidance, enabling the LLM to generate more in-
depth responses. These limitations underscore the
insufficiency of single-phase, query-as-is retrieval
paradigms in addressing real-world SCQ and ACQ,
challenging current graph-based RAG methods.
In this paper, we propose PankRAG, which
resolves queries through a hierarchical resolu-
tion path with global awareness, augmented by a
dependency-aware reranking mechanism. Specif-
ically, our approach leverages the robust plan-
ning capabilities of LLMs to generate globally-
aware (i.e., holistically planned rather than step-
wise) problem-solving pathways, explicitly reveal-
ing parallel and progressive relationships among
sub-questions. Next, we refine complex queries
by integrating the implicit critical information re-
solved from prior sub-question resolutions. This
dual-level process produces a hierarchical resolu-
tion pathway with global awareness, enhancing
interpretability through contextual grounding. It
enables LLMs to dynamically disambiguate and
contextualize ambiguous or polysemous queries
before final answer generation.
During execu-
tion, our dependency-aware reranking mechanism
augments and reorders retrieved content for sub-
questions dependent on prior resolutions by in-
tegrating their resolved dependencies. This pro-
cess refines the retrieved context by integrating
resolved sub-question dependencies, thereby en-
hancing the alignment between retrieved contexts
and dependency-driven sub-question answers chal-
lenging for LLMs to capture and resolve.
We evaluate PankRAG on four benchmark
datasets for ACQ and two for SCQ, demonstrat-
ing that it consistently outperforms state-of-the-art
baselines across six benchmarks. Compared to
NaiveRAG, GraphRAG, and LightRAG, PankRAG
achieves average win rates of 87.0%, 59.78% and
61.59% respectively in ACQ datasets, and achieves
average metric improvements of 17.42%, 11.61%,
11.08% in SCQ datasets.
Our contributions to this work are as follows:
• We propose a hierarchical query decom-
position strategy that systematically plans
globally-aware problem-solving pathways, en-
hancing LLMs’ capacity to capture and re-
solve implicit information and relationships,
fostering alignment between query intent and
generated responses.
• We propose a dependency-aware reranking
mechanism that augments and reorders re-
trieved content for the current sub-question
by integrating resolved sub-question depen-
dencies.
• PankRAG demonstrates consistent superior-
ity over state-of-the-art baselines, achieving
comprehensive improvements on four ACQ
benchmarks evaluated across six dimensions
and two SCQ benchmarks assessed via four
metrics.

Releated Works
2.1
Retrieval-Augmented Generation Based
On Chunks
RAG (Fan et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024) is widely
used to address the timeliness of knowledge bases
and hallucination issues (Yao et al., 2023) in large
language models (LLMs). Additionally, this ap-
proach is more cost-effective and easier to imple-
ment compared to methods like fine-tuning or re-
training, significantly reducing computational and
time resources. NaiveRAG (Gao et al., 2024) is
a method that typically processes documents and
queries by splitting them into smaller text chunks,
converting these chunks into vector representations.
The retrieved chunks, which are selected by com-
paring similarity with queries in vector space, are
then used to generate responses through LLMs.

Numerous advanced RAG methods, such as
MemoRAG (Qian et al., 2024), RQ-RAG (Chan
et al., 2024), and others (Asai et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
2023), build upon the foundational framework of
NaiveRAG, introducing specialized improvements.
MemoRAG (Qian et al., 2024) is an advanced
RAG framework empowered by global memory-
augmented retrieval. It combines two specialized
components: a lightweight system that generates
draft answers as "global memory" to guide pre-
cise retrieval, and a powerful system that crafts the
final answer using the retrieved information. RQ-
RAG is a method designed to tackle ambiguous
or complex queries. The model is equipped with
explicit capabilities for query rewriting, decom-
position, and disambiguation to enhance retrieval
accuracy. Inspired by two methods, our proposed
method integrates query decomposition and query
rewriting principles, enhancing comprehension of
hidden critical information or relationships within
queries.
However, most text-based RAG methods utilize
a flat structured database, failing to capture criti-
cal inter-chunk relationships, which constrains the
quality of LLM-generated responses. While ad-
vanced RAG variants improve generation accuracy,
their reliance on retrieving a limited subset of doc-
uments restricts comprehensive global information
integration. It ultimately hinders their ability to
address tasks requiring full-context understanding,
such as ACQ.
2.2
Retrieval-Augmented Generation Based
On KGs
Apart from a database of texts, a knowledge
graph (Jin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2020), a struc-
tured framework organizing knowledge through
interconnected entities and relationships, is increas-
ingly employed as a valuable source for RAG meth-
ods. Unlike traditional RAG (Gao et al., 2024),
Graph-based RAG methods (Sun et al., 2023; Ma
et al., 2024)leverage graphs either as knowledge
carriers or indexing tools to enable efficient re-
trieval. By modeling relationships between nodes,
they facilitate the discovery of topic-centered or
entity-centered knowledge pieces, ensuring thor-
ough and contextually coherent retrieval. Further-
more, the graph structure contained dependencies
or relationships between entities, which enhances
both precision and scalability compared to flat

databases.
Graph-based RAG methods differ in how they
leverage the underlying graph data. GraphRAG
(Edge et al., 2024) is designed to enhance ques-
tion answering over private text corpora by using
a knowledge graph. It pre-generates community
summaries for clusters of related entities and dy-
namically aggregates them into coherent final re-
sponses during query resolution. LightRAG (Guo
et al., 2024) is a novel framework that integrates
graph structures into text indexing and retrieval.
Employing a dual-level retrieval mechanism, it en-
hances comprehensive knowledge discovery and
accelerates response generation without compro-
mising contextual accuracy.
While prior research introduces knowledge
graphs as a structured database, inaccuracies in
the interpretation of hidden critical information
or relationships within queries remain persistent
challenges. Specifically, while knowledge graphs
provide RAG systems with robust knowledge rep-
resentation and enhanced retrieval performance,
their practical effectiveness depends on accurately
comprehending latent critical information and rela-
tionships within queries to identify precise retrieval
targets. Our proposed framework is specifically
designed to address this challenge. We also notice
a concurrent work, CogGRAG (Cheng et al., 2025)
that leverages the principle of query decomposi-
tion. While they predominantly focus on problem
decomposition strategy, our research prioritizes the
acquisition of problem-solving pathways, with par-
ticular emphasis on the interplay between parallel
interrelationships within these pathways and the
impact of resolved sub-question dependencies on
contextualizing information retrieved for the cur-
rent sub-question.
3
Methodology
In this section, we detail our proposed framework,
which comprises three core components. First,
Graph Construction extracts entities and relation-
ships from external knowledge sources to build
a structured knowledge graph. Next, Globally-
Aware Planning formulates a query resolution
pathway by identifying latent critical information
and relationships, improving LLMs’ comprehen-
siveness of complex queries. Finally, during Ex-
ecution, the LLM processes sub-questions either
concurrently or sequentially based on their inter-
relationships. After retrieving context for a sub-
question, the dependency-aware reranking mech-
anism augments and validates the content when
the current sub-question depends on a prior sub-
question. The LLM then leverages this refined
context to generate accurate responses. The overall
workflow of FG-RAG is shown in Figure 1.

We propose a hierarchical query decomposition
strategy that synergizes bottom-up relationship de-
composition with top-down contextual rephrasing
to address both implicit parallel or progress rela-
tionships and ambiguous or polysemous expres-
sions in complex queries. Unlike conventional it-
erative approaches (e.g., Chain-of-Thought, CoT),
which suffer from fixed iteration counts and myopic
decomposition prone to local optima, our strategy
dynamically adjusts the granularity and sequence
of reasoning steps based on query complexity.
In the bottom-up phase, two crucial relation-
ship types are detected. First, parallelizable task
relationships are identified to enable concurrent
resolution of mutually independent sub-questions,
optimizing computational efficiency by reducing
redundant resource contention and minimizing la-
tency. Second, latent dependencies are systemati-
cally extracted to ensure prerequisite sub-questions
are solved before dependent tasks.
Simultaneously, the top-down phase disam-
biguates polysemous expressions into logically co-
herent substructures, combining the resolved sub-
question dependencies. This dual mechanism re-
duces the risk of deviation in reasoning paths and
enhances computational efficiency by eliminating
redundant iterations, achieving a balanced trade-off
between precision and scalability. The detailed
workflow is presented below, and a real-world
query in Multihop-RAG dataset example is above
in Figure 2.
• Bottom-up phase: In the Parallel Relation-
ship Detection step, parallel semantic rela-
tionships—such as independent sub-questions
or co-occurring entities—are identified. If
no parallelism is detected, a single resolu-
tion sequence is initialized.
When paral-
lelism exists, multiple concurrent sequences
(SubQ1.x and SubQ2.x in Figure 2) are adap-
tively spawned to optimize concurrent execu-
tion. Concurrently, latent progress relation-
ships are extracted from the query.
Upon
identifying distinct relationships within the
query, sub-questions are generated and for-
malized into a directed acyclic graph (DAG),
where nodes represent sub-questions and
edges encode their relationships.
Finally,
topological sorting is applied to enforce
prerequisite-resolution order, ensuring logical
coherence during execution.
• Top-down phase: For queries containing
explicit ambiguities or polysemous expres-
sions, a disambiguation sequence combining
resolved sub-question dependencies (SubQ0.x
in Figure 2) is invoked. In this phase, an LLM-
driven rephrasing process converts ambigu-
ous queries into logically partitioned formula-
tions.
3.3
Execution
3.3.1
Retrieval
Following GraphRAG, we apply distinct retrieval
strategies for different query types.
For SCQ, we use a local search approach that
employs vector similarity metrics to identify nodes
explicitly referenced in the query, then expands
to neighboring nodes to retrieve related relation-
ships and community context. This method ensures
precise grounding in entity-centric evidence while
maintaining low computational overhead.
For ACQ, we perform hierarchical commu-
nity retrieval, dynamically selecting between
leaf-level summaries for detailed information and
higher-level summaries for thematic overviews.
This multi-scale strategy balances breadth and
depth to search for relevant information for com-
plex queries.
3.3.2
Dependency-Aware Reranking
Mechanism
The dependency-aware reranking mechanism is
triggered when resolving a sub-question that de-
pends on previously resolved sub-questions. It dy-
namically adjusts retrieval priorities by indirectly
leveraging these resolved dependencies. Unlike
conventional methods that directly incorporate re-
solved sub-question dependencies, risking cumula-
tive hallucination errors across iterative reasoning
steps, our mechanism mitigates such propagation
by reinforcing contextually relevant content and
filtering out incongruent evidence through seman-
tic similarity validation. If no dependencies are
present, the generation proceeds without reranking,
preserving computational efficiency while main-
taining robustness against cascading inaccuracies.
The reranking mechanism computes a composite
score by weighted summation of two components:
the intrinsic quality score Ri of retrieved content
and the semantic similarity Mi between retrieved
content and resolved dependent sub-question an-
swers. The intrinsic quality score Ri of retrieved
content is generated and retained during the orig-
inal retrieval phase (Edge et al., 2024).

Experiment
4.1
Experimental Setup
4.1.1
Datasets
We adopt the experimental framework of recent
graph-based RAG studies and evaluate our method
on six real-world datasets, encompassing both SCQ
and ACQ. We use the Musique and MultiHop-
RAG datasets for SCQ, which require reasoning
across multiple passages, understanding implicit
relationships, and synthesizing information. ACQ
demands holistic curation, multi-perspective syn-
thesis, and coherent organization of information at
scale. We evaluate our method on the UltraDomin
dataset (Qin et al., 2024), which is drawn from
428 college-level textbooks across 18 domains, se-
lecting the four domains used (Agriculture, Legal,
Computer Science, and Mix), whose detailed in-
formation is as follows in LightRAG’s evaluation.
We follow the standardized preprocessing pipelines
from GraphRAG and LightRAG.
4.1.2
Baselines
We compare PathRAG with the following state-of-
the-art methods across all datasets:
• NaiveRAG (Gao et al., 2024): In this RAG
method, text is divided into chunks, stored
as embeddings in a vector database, and the
most semantically similar chunks are retrieved
during queries to enable efficient matching.
• GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024): In this graph-
based RAG method, they hierarchically orga-
nize knowledge through entity-level descrip-
tions and clustered community summaries.
For global queries, it retrieves community-
focused knowledge and aggregates related en-
tities to produce comprehensive responses.

Hyperparameter Analysis
The parameters α and β are designed to optimize
the original quality score by integrating a novel
similarity metric, constrained by α + β = 1 to en-
sure the normalized score range. Experimental
results demonstrate that the optimal parameters
for SCQ are α > 0.6 β < 0.4, whereas for ACQ,
α = 0.75 and β = 0.25 , with both configurations
maximizing retrieval performance.
In SCQ datasets, we evaluated retrieval perfor-
mance across various (α:β) ratios in Figure 4.
As (α:β) increases, key metrics—including an-
swer relevance, context recall, and context preci-
sion—monotonically improve, peaking at (α:β) =
0.6:0.4, before declining. Although faithfulness
attains its maximum at (α:β) = 0.4:0.6, its value at
0.6 : 0.4 is only 0.0027 lower, indicating a negligi-
ble trade-off. Thus, α = 0.6 and β = 0.4 represent
the optimal balance. We indicate that when α >
0.6 β < 0.4, the weight on sub-question answers is
insufficient to adequately reinforce and validate re-
trieved context. Conversely, when α < 0.6 β > 0.4,
over-emphasis on sub-question answers introduces
negative hallucinations that outweigh positive re-
inforcement, degrading performance. At α = 0.6
and β = 0.4, these opposing effects are balanced,
yielding the best overall retrieval results.
In ACQ datasets, We adjust a pair of hyperpa-
rameters on the Mix and Agriculture dataset, and
then calculate the win rates compared with Ligh-
tRAG, the best baseline. As shown in Figure 3,
parameter configuration of α = 0.75 and β = 0.25
achieves the highest win rates across the majority of
the six dimensions and secures the second-highest
performance in all remaining cases. For ACQ, the
optimal parameter configuration that maximizes
retrieval performance is achieved when α = 0.75
and β = 0.25.

5
Conclusion
In this paper, we propose PankRAG, a novel RAG
framework designed to enhance LLMs’ capac-
ity to accurately interpret and retrieve informa-
tion for complex queries. PankRAG combines a
globally aware pathway-resolution module with a
dependency-aware reranking mechanism. By exe-
cuting globally coherent reasoning pathways and
augmenting retrieved content through dependency-
aware reranking, our framework captures and re-
solves hidden relationships and information in both
abstract and specific complex queries, significantly
enhancing retrieval performance and response qual-
ity.
Limitations
While PankRAG enhances retrieval accuracy and
comprehensiveness through globally-aware query
resolution paths and dependency-aware reranking
mechanisms, it faces two key limitations. First,
resolving multiple sub-problems during retrieval
necessitates additional LLM invocations, which
improves quality of response but introduces higher
computational overhead. Second, our evaluation
follows the relative metrics of existing graph-based
RAG methods, which may lack stability compared
to absolute benchmarks. Future work will focus on
establishing more reliable and absolute evaluation
metrics tailored to graph-based RAG frameworks.
